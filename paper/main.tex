\begin{abstract}
Facial images are increasingly used in studies that require grouping, comparing, or auditing human representations. Yet, many available approaches rely either on human annotation, which can introduce subjective and socially sensitive judgments, or on opaque embeddings whose decision mechanisms are difficult to inspect. This paper introduces the Segmented and Interpretable Facial Proportion Metric (SI-FPM), a facial representation built from segmented region areas and pairwise proportional ratios. SI-FPM converts facial segments into a compact vector of interpretable geometric relations, enabling scale-normalized comparison between individuals and reference groups without requiring facial landmarks or learned embeddings. We further propose an explainability layer that identifies, for each proximity decision, the specific named facial ratios that most strongly drive proximity to an operational reference group. The method is evaluated through three complementary analyses: identity-oriented similarity across image sets of the same individual, group-level distributional auditing on the IMDB-WIKI dataset, and a generated-image case study covering four generative AI models across two occupational prompts. SI-FPM produces directionally correct group-proximity assignments in all generated-image panels without labeled training signal, with proximity gaps ranging from 0.11 to 0.43 across models. Throughout, dataset-provided labels are treated strictly as operational annotations for aggregate analysis and do not represent claims about gender identity, biological sex, or individual self-identification.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{introduction}

The increasing use of facial images in computational studies has created a methodological tension. On the one hand, researchers often need to compare images, evaluate group-level patterns, or audit visual representations produced by generative systems. On the other hand, many of these analyses involve socially sensitive categories, such as gender, ethnicity, or other appearance-related group labels, where direct human labeling can introduce subjective, culturally situated, and potentially harmful judgments \cite{buolamwini2018gender,mehrabi2021survey,heidari2016sager}. Fully automated deep models may reduce manual annotation costs and often represent faces or images through high-dimensional embeddings \cite{Schroff_2015_CVPR,Deng_2019_CVPR}. However, because these embeddings are not organized around human-interpretable facial attributes, it can be difficult to determine which visual cues support a decision \cite{doshivelez2017rigorousscienceinterpretablemachine,rudin2019stop,burrell2016machine}.

The state of the art in facial image analysis has moved from early deep verification systems to highly discriminative embedding-based representations. These methods produce latent coordinates optimized for discriminative power rather than for correspondence with named facial attributes, enabling accurate similarity scores without a direct account of which observable facial proportions contributed to the comparison. The Related Work section reviews this trajectory in detail; the key point for SI-FPM's motivation is that discriminative embeddings are not organized around human-interpretable facial features, making audit-oriented inspection of their decisions structurally difficult.

In parallel, the literature on AI fairness and representation has shown that visual systems can encode uneven demographic performance, stereotyped associations, and culturally narrow depictions of people \cite{buolamwini2018gender,mehrabi2021survey,luccioni2023stablebiasanalyzingsocietal, franco2026bias}. This is particularly relevant for generative AI, where synthetic portraits may be used as evidence of social representation despite being produced by systems trained on large and uneven visual corpora. Interpretability research has therefore argued for methods whose decisions can be inspected, contested, and evaluated in relation to meaningful features rather than only post hoc explanations of black-box models \cite{doshivelez2017rigorousscienceinterpretablemachine,rudin2019stop}. For studies involving sensitive labels, this creates a need for methods that can support group-level auditing while making their assumptions and feature contributions explicit.

This paper addresses that tension by proposing the Segmented and Interpretable Facial Proportion Metric (SI-FPM), a facial metric designed for transparent image comparison and representation auditing. Instead of treating facial images as opaque embeddings, SI-FPM extracts segmented facial regions and converts them into pairwise proportional ratios. The resulting vector is not intended to define human identity, gender, ethnicity, or any other social category. Rather, it provides an interpretable geometric representation that can be used to compare images against explicitly defined reference groups under a controlled experimental protocol.

The central motivation of SI-FPM is interpretability. In many audit settings, the relevant question is not only whether an image is closer to one reference group than another, but also which measurable visual proportions contributed most to that proximity. For this reason, we pair the metric with a feature-wise explanation layer that ranks local proportional drivers. These drivers do not describe essential characteristics of social groups. They identify, within a dataset and a specific reference comparison, which facial ratios most reduced the distance to one operational group and increased the distance to the contrast group.

This distinction is especially important for studies involving gender- or ethnicity-related labels. Gender is fluid, multidimensional, socially mediated, and not reducible to facial appearance. Ethnicity and race are likewise historically and culturally constructed categories, not biological essences that can be inferred from facial proportions. In this work, labels such as male and female are treated only as dataset-provided operational annotations. In the text, we refer to them as Group A and Group B whenever discussing the methodological machinery, and we clarify when figures use the original dataset labels for readability. Future ethnicity-related uses of SI-FPM should be understood in the same audit-oriented sense: as analysis of representation patterns under predefined reference labels, not as classification of persons into essential categories.

The contributions of this paper are fourfold. First, we introduce SI-FPM as a segmented facial proportion metric that represents facial images through scale-normalized pairwise ratios. Second, we define a group-comparison procedure that supports both identity-oriented similarity analysis and aggregate representation auditing. Third, we propose a driver-based explanation layer that identifies the top facial ratios responsible for a group-proximity decision. Fourth, we provide an operational ethical framing that distinguishes audit-oriented group proximity from individual-level classification, establishing the reference-label and interpretive conditions under which the method can be applied responsibly.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section \ref{related_work} reviews work on facial measurements, segmentation-based visual analysis, bias auditing, and explainable representations. Section \ref{methodology} presents the ethical framing, the SI-FPM formulation, and the feature-level explanation procedure. Section \ref{results_discussion} reports the identity-oriented, group-level, and generated-image analyses, followed by a discussion of ethical implications and limitations. Section \ref{conclusion} concludes the paper.


\section{Related Work}
\label{related_work}

\subsection{Facial Representation Learning and Recognition}

The computational analysis of facial images has followed a trajectory from compact statistical representations toward increasingly powerful but increasingly opaque embedding spaces. Early eigenface approaches established the viability of dimensionality-reduced face descriptors derived from principal component analysis of pixel intensities \cite{turk1991eigenfaces},
% NEW REF NEEDED: Turk, M. & Pentland, A. (1991). Eigenfaces for Recognition. Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience, 3(1), 71–86. DOI:10.1162/jocn.1991.3.1.71
enabling comparison and retrieval within an explicitly parametrized subspace. Active Appearance Models extended this by jointly modeling shape and texture variation, producing face representations whose coordinates correspond to named modes of facial change \cite{cootes2001aam}. These early methods were inspectable by design, but their representational scope was limited by the expressiveness of the underlying statistical model.

The arrival of large-scale deep convolutional networks shifted the methodological center of face analysis decisively toward discriminative embeddings. DeepFace demonstrated near-human face verification performance on the Labeled Faces in the Wild (LFW) benchmark \cite{taigman2014deepface,huang2008labeled},
% NEW REF NEEDED: Huang, G. et al. (2008). Labeled Faces in the Wild: A Database for Studying Face Recognition in Unconstrained Environments. University of Massachusetts, Amherst, Technical Report 07-49.
and FaceNet formalized a triplet-loss paradigm in which facial images are mapped to compact vectors that are directly optimized for verification, recognition, and clustering \cite{schroff2015facenet}. These systems made facial comparison scalable and highly effective but replaced named spatial measurements with high-dimensional coordinates whose meaning is opaque with respect to observable facial properties.

Subsequent work concentrated on refining the geometry and loss structure of the feature space. SphereFace introduced multiplicative angular margins on a unit hypersphere, CosFace proposed an additive cosine margin for improved class separation, and ArcFace adopted an additive angular margin with a direct geodesic interpretation, establishing one of the most widely used face recognition paradigms \cite{liu2017sphereface,wang2018cosface,deng2019arcface}. Further advances addressed sample difficulty, image quality, and margin flexibility: CurricularFace progressively increased the difficulty of training samples, MagFace linked embedding magnitude to face quality, AdaFace adapted the margin based on estimated image quality, and ElasticFace introduced stochastic margin variation to improve generalization \cite{huang2020curricularface,meng2021magface,kim2022adaface,boutros2022elasticface}. The role of data scale and curation was studied systematically in WebFace260M, which showed that both dataset size and annotation quality substantially govern recognition performance \cite{zhu2021webface260m}.

Beyond face-specific systems, large-scale vision–language pretraining has produced versatile and semantically broad visual representations. CLIP aligned visual and textual encoders through contrastive learning over hundreds of millions of image–text pairs, enabling zero-shot transfer and semantic retrieval across visual domains \cite{pmlr-v139-radford21a}. More recent foundation models such as DINOv2 demonstrated that self-supervised vision-transformer representations pretrained on large curated collections generalize strongly to face analysis tasks without explicit recognition training \cite{oquab2023dinov2}.
% NEW REF NEEDED: Oquab, M. et al. (2023). DINOv2: Learning Robust Visual Features without Supervision. arXiv:2304.07193. TMLR 2024.
Despite their representational power, none of these systems expose which facial proportions or named regions underlie a similarity decision. SI-FPM is therefore complementary rather than competitive: it trades discriminative power for transparency, targeting audit contexts in which an inspectable, lower-dimensional representation tied to named facial measurements is preferable to an accurate but opaque embedding.

\subsection{Bias, Fairness, and Visual Representation Auditing}

Research on algorithmic fairness has established that machine learning systems can produce systematically unequal outcomes across demographic groups, and that these disparities reflect choices embedded in data collection, labeling practices, model design, and evaluation protocols \cite{mehrabi2021survey}. Foundational work in natural language processing showed that distributed word representations encode human-like gender and racial stereotypes \cite{bolukbasi2016debiasing},
% NEW REF NEEDED: Bolukbasi, T. et al. (2016). Man is to Computer Programmer as Woman is to Homemaker? Debiasing Word Embeddings. NeurIPS 2016. arXiv:1607.06520.
establishing that bias can enter a system at the representation level rather than only through explicit classification objectives. Subsequent research demonstrated that such biases propagate into downstream visual recognition and generation pipelines.

For facial analysis specifically, Gender Shades provided one of the first systematic empirical audits of commercial systems, documenting large intersectional accuracy disparities that disproportionately affected darker-skinned women \cite{buolamwini2018gender}. NIST's Face Recognition Vendor Test extended this analysis to a broad evaluation of demographic effects across recognition algorithms at scale \cite{grother2019frvt}. Dataset-level responses included Racial Faces in the Wild, which benchmarked recognition performance across racial groups, and FairFace, which provided balanced annotations of race, gender, and age for bias evaluation and mitigation \cite{wang2019rfw,karkkainen2021fairface}. Broader surveys have emphasized that these disparities are not mere technical artifacts: they reflect socially constructed labeling decisions, historically uneven data collection, and deployment contexts that amplify existing inequalities \cite{mehrabi2021survey}. The SAGER guidelines have argued for more consistent documentation and ethical handling of sex and gender variables in research designs, a principle this paper applies to its operational label framing \cite{heidari2016sager}.

The emergence of large-scale generative image models has introduced a complementary dimension to representation auditing. Rather than evaluating classifier accuracy disparities, generative audits examine whether synthesized imagery reflects stereotyped or systematically unequal social representations. Stable Bias examined distributional patterns in diffusion model outputs across gender identities and professional roles, while Naik and Nushi analyzed occupational, personality, and geographic biases in images generated by DALL-E 2 and Stable Diffusion \cite{luccioni2023stablebias,naik2023socialbiases}. T2IAT adapted the logic of implicit association testing to text-to-image generation, measuring valence and stereotypical associations in synthesized outputs \cite{wang2023t2iat}. OpenBias proposed a framework for detecting open-set biases without requiring predefined category lists, and Girrbach et al.\ conducted a large-scale gender bias evaluation across millions of generated images and everyday prompts \cite{dinca2024openbias,girrbach2025largescale}. Franco et al.\ extended this line of work by comparing cross-country demographic representations in generative AI against geographically grounded population baselines, showing that biases compound across cultural and regional specificity \cite{franco2026biasbeyondborders}. Recent studies have further shown that biases in generated imagery operate along emotional and appearance-related dimensions beyond binary gender labels \cite{bianchi2023easily}.
% NEW REF NEEDED: Bianchi, F. et al. (2023). Easily Accessible Text-to-Image Generation Amplifies Demographic Stereotypes at Large Scale. ACM FAccT 2023. DOI:10.1145/3593013.3594095. (Verify key: bianchi2023easily)

These studies collectively motivate SI-FPM in two respects. First, they establish that visual representation auditing is a legitimate and methodologically active research area in which the choice of analytical tool matters. Second, they expose a persistent explanatory gap: most existing audits assign categories through external classifiers, vision-language models, or human annotation, and few directly expose which concrete visual features drive the audit result. SI-FPM addresses this gap by making every feature a named facial proportion and by reporting feature-level drivers for group-proximity decisions, making the assumptions and visual cues of an audit operationally transparent.

\subsection{Facial Anthropometry and Proportional Measurement}

The systematic quantification of facial proportions has a long history across art, medicine, and forensic science. Classical neoclassical canons divided the face into ideal horizontal thirds and vertical fifths, establishing reference proportions that persist in aesthetic medicine and surgical planning. Quantitative craniofacial anthropometry formalized these intuitions through direct measurement at anatomical landmarks, producing normative databases of facial distances, angles, and areas across age, sex, and ancestry \cite{farkas1994anthropometry}.
% NEW REF NEEDED: Farkas, L.G. (Ed.) (1994). Anthropometry of the Head and Face (2nd ed.). Raven Press. ISBN: 978-0-7817-0159-4. (Verify key: farkas1994anthropometry)
Empirical assessments of the golden ratio hypothesis have shown that, while individual aesthetic preference varies, specific facial proportion configurations are associated with higher perceived attractiveness in controlled psychophysical studies \cite{pallett2010golden}.
% NEW REF NEEDED: Pallett, P.M., Link, S., & Lee, K. (2010). New "golden" ratios for facial beauty. Vision Research, 50(2), 149–154. DOI:10.1016/j.visres.2009.11.003. (Verify key: pallett2010golden)

Proportional facial measurement has also found clinical utility. Facial landmark-based metrics have been applied to characterize phenotypic syndromes in which systematic deviations from reference distributions carry diagnostic relevance, as in craniofacial dysmorphology and genetic syndrome identification \cite{hammond2007dysmorphology,gurovich2019predicting}.
% NEW REF NEEDED: Hammond, P. (2007). The use of 3D face shape modelling in dysmorphology. Archives of Disease in Childhood, 92(12), 1120–1126. DOI:10.1136/adc.2006.103507. (Verify key: hammond2007dysmorphology)
% NEW REF NEEDED: Gurovich, Y. et al. (2019). Identifying facial phenotypes of genetic disorders using deep learning. Nature Medicine, 25, 60–64. DOI:10.1038/s41591-018-0279-0. (Verify key: gurovich2019predicting)
These medical and forensic uses demonstrate that proportional measurements can be meaningful descriptors for aggregate population studies while remaining far from deterministic at the individual level — a distinction that SI-FPM explicitly preserves in its audit framing.

In the computer vision literature, geometric and proportional facial descriptors appeared early but were largely overshadowed by learned features in the deep learning era. More recently, there has been renewed interest in explicit proportion-based representations as interpretable baselines in visual ethics contexts, where named feature measurements allow researchers to inspect what an auditing system responds to rather than relying solely on post hoc explanations of opaque scores. SI-FPM contributes to this renewal by grounding proportional measurement in segmented facial regions rather than sparse landmark coordinates. This design extends the anthropometric tradition to an area-based representation that is applicable when precise landmark detection is unavailable, scale-independent by construction, and amenable to group-level distributional comparison under clearly documented reference conditions.

\subsection{Interpretable and Geometry-Based Facial Analysis}

A parallel line of work has represented faces through explicit geometric structure rather than learned embeddings. Active Appearance Models combined statistical shape and texture variation into a parametric face model whose coordinates correspond to recognizable modes of facial change \cite{cootes2001aam}. Three-Dimensional Morphable Models represented facial shape and reflectance through parametric 3D face spaces, enabling controlled synthesis and shape-based analysis whose outputs retain geometric meaning \cite{blanz1999morphable}. Landmark-based methods provided a more tractable operational form of explicit geometry: Kazemi and Sullivan demonstrated that an ensemble of regression trees could predict 68 canonical facial landmarks in under one millisecond, and Bulat and Tzimiropoulos showed that deep alignment networks could match or exceed this precision across 2D and 3D datasets \cite{kazemi2014facealignment,bulat2017facealignment}. These methods are more inspectable than generic embeddings because their outputs correspond to identifiable points or shape parameters, though they remain dependent on the stability and semantic coverage of the landmark set.

Subsequent work expanded the density, robustness, and realism of facial geometry estimation. MediaPipe Face Mesh introduced a dense 468-point mesh estimated in real time from monocular video \cite{kartynnik2019facemesh}, and Attention Mesh improved high-fidelity prediction for semantically important regions such as eyes and lips through attention-weighted refinement \cite{grishchenko2020attentionmesh}. 3DDFA-V2 targeted fast, accurate, and stable dense 3D alignment through a cascaded regression framework, while DECA and EMOCA advanced monocular 3D face capture by jointly modeling detailed expression and emotion-related geometry \cite{guo2020threeddfa,feng2021deca,danecek2022emoca}. These methods demonstrate that explicit facial geometry remains important in the deep learning era, especially in settings where the goal is to preserve structural information or support downstream geometric reasoning.

Foundation segmentation has opened a complementary pathway that does not depend on predefined landmark coordinates. The Segment Anything Model demonstrated that prompt-guided segmentation generalizes to arbitrary object categories from large-scale pretraining, supporting region-level mask generation without per-task retraining \cite{kirillov2023segment}. Text-grounded variants, including LangSAM, extend this to free-form language prompts, enabling the extraction of semantically named facial regions such as the nose, mouth, eyes, and chin from descriptive inputs. Once region masks are available, area-based proportional measurements become directly computable without landmark detection. This is the technical pathway adopted by SI-FPM: segmented regions replace landmarks as the primary input, and pairwise area ratios replace distance-based measurements, preserving interpretability because every feature remains tied to a named anatomical region.

The broader interpretability literature further grounds this design. Doshi-Velez and Kim argued for a rigorous science of interpretable machine learning in which the claim of interpretability is formally evaluated rather than assumed \cite{doshivelez2017rigorousscienceinterpretablemachine}. Rudin contended that, in high-stakes or socially sensitive decision-making, inherently interpretable models should be preferred over post hoc explanations of black-box systems, because the latter can fail to faithfully represent the model's actual reasoning \cite{rudin2019stop}. Burrell examined structural sources of opacity in machine learning, distinguishing opacity arising from model complexity, institutional secrecy, and the fundamental mismatch between mathematical operations and human-meaningful concepts \cite{burrell2016machine}. SI-FPM is designed to be interpretable by construction rather than by approximation: each feature has a semantic name, each proximity score decomposes into named ratio contributions, and no post hoc surrogate model is required to produce an explanation. This places SI-FPM within the tradition of inherently interpretable models \cite{rudin2019stop} and extends that tradition to the domain of facial representation auditing.


\section{Methodology}
\label{methodology}

\subsection{Operational Definitions and Ethical Framing}
\label{operational_definitions}

SI-FPM is proposed as an audit-oriented metric, not as a system for assigning social identity to individuals. This distinction guides the terminology used throughout the paper.

We use the term \emph{reference group} to denote a collection of images associated with a label provided by a dataset, a study protocol, or an explicitly defined audit baseline. In the experiments involving the IMDB-WIKI dataset, the available labels are male and female. For the methodological sections, we refer to these as Group A and Group B to emphasize that the algorithm operates on reference distributions, not on ontological categories. When figure labels use male/female for visual clarity, they should be read strictly as dataset annotations.

We use the term \emph{group proximity} rather than identity assignment. A test image may be closer to Group A than Group B under SI-FPM, but this means only that its segmented facial proportion vector is more similar to the reference distribution of Group A under the chosen metric. It does not imply that the depicted person belongs to that group, identifies with that group, or can be socially or biologically classified by the system.

The same restriction applies to possible future ethnicity-related applications. Ethnicity and race are complex social and historical categories whose meaning varies across countries, cultures, and institutional contexts. SI-FPM may support aggregate audits of visual representation patterns when reference labels are externally defined and ethically justified, but it should not be used to infer ethnicity or race at the individual level.

Identity-oriented experiments in this paper are treated separately. When SI-FPM is used to compare images of the same public figure, the goal is to evaluate whether facial proportion vectors retain enough stable geometric information for image-set similarity. This experiment does not support surveillance deployment or individual identification in unconstrained settings. It is included as a controlled test of representation consistency. With these operational definitions in place, Section~\ref{metric} formalizes the SI-FPM feature vector, the group-proximity measure, and the feature-level explanation layer.


\subsection{The SI-FPM Metric}
\label{metric}

SI-FPM represents a facial image through proportional relations between segmented facial regions. Let $\mathcal{R}=\{R_1,R_2,\dots,R_n\}$ be the set of facial regions extracted from an image, where each region corresponds to a semantically defined facial component — in the current implementation, comprising the face, nose, mouth, eyes, eyebrows, chin, and hair. Each region $R_i$ is represented by a polygon $P_i$ in the image plane, with vertices $\{v_{i,1},v_{i,2},\dots,v_{i,k_i}\}$.

The area of $P_i$ is computed using the standard polygon area formula:

\[
\text{Area}(P_i)=\frac{1}{2}\left|\sum_{t=1}^{k_i}x_{i,t}y_{i,t+1}-y_{i,t}x_{i,t+1}\right|,
\]

where $(x_{i,t},y_{i,t})$ are the coordinates of the $t$-th vertex and $(x_{i,k_i+1},y_{i,k_i+1})=(x_{i,1},y_{i,1})$ closes the polygon. For any pair of valid regions $(i,j)$, SI-FPM defines the facial proportion:

\[
r_{i,j}=\frac{\text{Area}(P_i)}{\text{Area}(P_j)}.
\]

Area is preferred over linear distance measurements for three reasons. First, the area of a segmented region captures its full two-dimensional spatial extent — reflecting both width and height simultaneously — whereas a landmark-based distance encodes only one linear dimension of the feature. Second, area estimates aggregate over the entire mask boundary, making them less sensitive to local perturbations at region edges than distance measures, which depend on the precise localization of exactly two points. Third, segmentation models such as SAM produce polygon masks as their primary output, making area computation a direct and parameter-free step that requires no additional landmark detection within each region.

The SI-FPM vector for image $x$ is the ordered collection of non-redundant pairwise ratios:

\[
\mathbf{s}(x)=[r_{1,2}, r_{1,3}, \dots, r_{i,j}, \dots, r_{n-1,n}].
\]

Because the vector is built from ratios, it is invariant to global image scaling. If all image coordinates are scaled by a factor $\alpha$, each polygon area is scaled by $\alpha^2$, and the corresponding ratio remains unchanged:

\[
r_{i,j}^{\alpha}=\frac{\alpha^2\text{Area}(P_i)}{\alpha^2\text{Area}(P_j)}=r_{i,j}.
\]

This property does not make SI-FPM invariant to all imaging conditions. Pose, expression, occlusion, segmentation quality, and perspective effects can still affect the measured regions. However, the ratio-based formulation reduces dependence on absolute image size and makes each feature directly interpretable.


\subsubsection{Reference Groups and Proximity Scores}
\label{score_calc}

Let $\mathcal{G}$ be a reference group containing $m$ images with SI-FPM vectors $\{\mathbf{s}_1,\mathbf{s}_2,\dots,\mathbf{s}_m\}$. A group prototype can be defined as the component-wise mean:

\[
\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\mathcal{G}}=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=1}^{m}\mathbf{s}_k,
\]

or, in robustness-oriented settings, as the component-wise median. The experiments reported in this paper use the component-wise mean; the median variant is available for datasets with anticipated outliers or heavily skewed ratio distributions. For a target image $x$, group proximity can be measured through cosine similarity:

\[
C(x,\mathcal{G})=\frac{\mathbf{s}(x)\cdot\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\mathcal{G}}}{\|\mathbf{s}(x)\|\|\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\mathcal{G}}\|}.
\]

Cosine similarity is preferred over Euclidean distance because all SI-FPM features are positive proportional ratios, and the absolute magnitude of $\mathbf{s}(x)$ reflects the composition of available segmented regions rather than a facial property of interest. By normalizing both vectors, cosine similarity captures the alignment of the proportional profile across regions, making the comparison invariant to differences in the number of valid ratios recovered per image.

For two reference groups, $\mathcal{G}_A$ and $\mathcal{G}_B$, a target image can be assigned to the group with higher proximity:

\[
\hat{g}(x)=\arg\max_{g\in\{A,B\}} C(x,\mathcal{G}_g).
\]

This assignment should be read as a proximity decision under the chosen feature space, not as a social or biological classification. In experiments using male/female dataset labels, Group A and Group B correspond to those dataset-provided annotations.


\subsubsection{Feature-Level Explanation}
\label{explainability}

The main interpretability advantage of SI-FPM is that each coordinate has a semantic name. To identify which features most support a group-proximity decision, we compute a driver score for each ratio. Let $m_{g,k}$ be the median value of feature $k$ in group $g$, and let $\text{IQR}_{g,k}$ be its interquartile range. The standardized distance between a target feature $s_k(x)$ and group $g$ is:

\[
D_{g,k}(x)=\frac{|s_k(x)-m_{g,k}|}{\text{IQR}_{g,k}+\epsilon}.
\]

We weight each feature by its between-group separability:

\[
w_k=\frac{|m_{A,k}-m_{B,k}|}{\text{IQR}_{A,B,k}+\epsilon},
\]

where $\text{IQR}_{A,B,k}$ is the interquartile range of the combined distribution of feature $k$ across both groups (i.e., computed over the concatenation of all values from $\mathcal{G}_A$ and $\mathcal{G}_B$), and $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$ avoids division by zero. The signed driver score favoring Group A over Group B is:

\[
E_k^{A\leftarrow B}(x)=w_k\left(D_{B,k}(x)-D_{A,k}(x)\right).
\]

Positive values indicate that feature $k$ brings the target image closer to Group A than to Group B; negative values indicate the reverse. The top-ranked absolute values are visualized as local drivers. These values should be interpreted as dataset- and comparison-specific evidence, not as universal traits of a social group.


\subsubsection{Implementation}
\label{code}

Prior to segmentation, each image is passed through a face detection step to localize the facial region and suppress background areas that could otherwise attract spurious mask assignments. The detected face region is cropped with a small contextual margin to retain adjacent structures such as the hairline and chin contour, which are relevant to several SI-FPM ratios. Images are processed at their original resolution without forced resizing, preserving the spatial information required for accurate polygon area computation. No facial alignment or landmark-based normalization is applied before segmentation, as the ratio formulation is scale-invariant and the segmentation model operates directly on the raw crop.

The current implementation uses LangSAM\footnote{\url{https://github.com/luca-medeiros/lang-segment-anything}}, built on SAM, to segment facial regions from text prompts. The target regions include the face, nose, mouth, eyes, eyebrows, chin, and hair when available. Left and right eye and eyebrow regions are segmented independently and their areas are summed to produce a single bilateral measurement when a combined region is required. If a segmentation mask is missing or unreliable, the corresponding ratio is stored as missing rather than forcing an artificial value. This prevents one failed segmentation from invalidating the full vector while preserving uncertainty in downstream analysis.

Ratios involving the hair region are marked with an asterisk and are included only when the corresponding mask is successfully segmented; in their absence the affected features are stored as missing values.

\section{Results and Discussion}
\label{results_discussion}

We evaluate SI-FPM through three complementary analyses. The first tests whether facial proportion vectors preserve enough individual consistency to compare image sets of the same public figure, using a curated collection of public-figure images. The second examines group-level proximity using male/female labels from the IMDB-WIKI dataset (690 images, treated as Group A and Group B operational annotations), providing quantitative distributional evidence across the full feature set. The third presents a qualitative generated-image audit for portraits produced by four generative AI models, illustrating the method's output in an applied AI ethics context. Together, the three analyses span consistency evaluation, distributional group auditing, and case-level explanation.


\subsection{Identity Identification}
\label{face_id}

The identity-oriented experiment evaluates representation consistency rather than deployment-ready identification. Each dataset corresponds to images of a widely recognized public figure, and the objective is to determine whether within-person SI-FPM similarity is higher than cross-person similarity.

For this analysis, we selected public figures with multiple available images: Pele, Serena Williams, Jackie Chan, Han Kang, Ayrton Senna, and Marie Curie. These examples were chosen to provide varied image sources, poses, ages, and visual contexts. SI-FPM vectors are computed for each image, and pairwise similarity is measured across image sets.

Let \(D_i\) and \(D_j\) represent two image sets associated with individuals \(i\) and \(j\). The cross-dataset similarity \(F_{i,j}\) is defined as the average cosine similarity between all valid SI-FPM vectors in the two sets:

\[
F_{i,j}=\frac{1}{|D_i||D_j|}\sum_{\mathbf{s}_a\in D_i}\sum_{\mathbf{s}_b\in D_j}\frac{\mathbf{s}_a\cdot\mathbf{s}_b}{\|\mathbf{s}_a\|\|\mathbf{s}_b\|}.
\]

The resulting matrix, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:cross-dataset-heatmap}, compares within-set and cross-set similarities. Diagonal values indicate consistency within the same public figure's image set, whereas off-diagonal values indicate similarity across different figures. This experiment is not presented as a biometric identification system. It is a controlled test of whether SI-FPM captures stable facial proportional structure across image variation.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{shpm_cross_dataset_heatmap.png}
    \caption{Cross-dataset similarity heatmap obtained with SI-FPM. Diagonal elements represent the cosine similarity of each individual's prototype vector with itself (always 1.00), while off-diagonal values indicate cross-individual prototype similarity.}
    \label{fig:cross-dataset-heatmap}
\end{figure}

The heatmap reveals a consistent and substantial gap between within-individual and cross-individual similarity scores. All diagonal entries equal 1.00 by definition, reflecting the cosine self-similarity of each individual's mean SI-FPM vector. Off-diagonal values range from 0.04 to 0.53, remaining well below this ceiling across all pairs. The highest cross-individual similarity is observed between Jackie Chan and Ayrton Senna (0.46--0.53), while the lowest pairs involve Marie Curie and Senna (0.04--0.06) and Jackie Chan and Marie Curie (0.07--0.09). These results suggest that SI-FPM proportion vectors, computed from segmented regions, retain enough individual-level geometric structure to maintain a measurable separation between different identities at the prototype level. The non-symmetric values observed in some off-diagonal pairs reflect differences in the composition and variability of each image set, since prototype vectors are sensitive to the distribution of images available for each individual. Taken together, the experiment supports the use of SI-FPM for consistency-oriented comparison of facial proportion representations, while acknowledging that the metric is not designed or evaluated for open-set identification under unconstrained conditions.


\subsection{Group-Level Audit with Dataset Labels}
\label{gender}

The second experiment evaluates whether SI-FPM can support group-level auditing when reference labels are already present in a dataset. We use the male/female labels provided by the IMDB-WIKI dataset as operational annotations. In the methodological interpretation, these are Group A and Group B. The working subset comprises 690 images — 351 female-labeled and 339 male-labeled — drawn from the IMDB-WIKI collection. The analysis does not attempt to infer gender identity or biological sex from images; it evaluates whether facial proportion vectors show measurable proximity patterns with respect to these existing dataset labels.

For each image, SI-FPM extracts facial ratios such as \texttt{Nose\_vs\_Face}, \texttt{Mouth\_vs\_Face}, \texttt{Eyes\_vs\_Face}, \texttt{Nose\_vs\_Mouth}, \texttt{Eyes\_vs\_Nose}, and \texttt{Eyes\_vs\_Mouth}. We then compare the distributions of these ratios between the two reference groups. Violin plots and box plots provide interpretable summaries of central tendency, dispersion, and overlap, while the t-SNE projection offers a qualitative view of the SI-FPM feature space.

The central point of this experiment is not that facial proportions determine gender. Rather, the experiment asks whether a transparent metric can reveal which proportional patterns are associated with dataset labels in aggregate. This is precisely the type of setting in which an interpretable audit method is useful: it allows researchers to inspect the variables involved in a group-level decision instead of delegating the analysis to an opaque classifier.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{violin_face_ratios_imdb_wiki.png}
    \caption{Violin plots of facial proportion distributions extracted with SI-FPM from the IMDB-WIKI dataset. The male/female labels are dataset-provided operational annotations and are interpreted in the text as Group A and Group B.}
    \label{fig:violin-face-ratios}
\end{figure}

The violin plots show the distributions of the three face-relative ratios across both reference groups. \texttt{Nose\_vs\_Face} displays nearly identical distributions for the two groups, with medians close to 0.06 and broadly overlapping shapes, indicating that nose area relative to total face area carries minimal group-discriminative information under the IMDB-WIKI annotations. \texttt{Mouth\_vs\_Face} exhibits a subtle rightward shift in the female-labeled group, suggesting a slightly larger mouth area relative to the face. The clearest separation among the three ratios appears in \texttt{Eyes\_vs\_Face}, where the female-labeled group shows both a higher median and a broader distribution, making eye area relative to total face area the most informative of these face-relative features.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering

    \caption{Box plots of cross-facial proportions computed with SI-FPM. The plots show how specific facial ratios vary across the two dataset-provided reference groups, emphasizing distributional overlap as well as differences in central tendency.}

The box plots extend the analysis to cross-region ratios and reveal sharper distributional contrasts. \texttt{Eyes\_vs\_Nose} shows the strongest separation observed across all ratios: the female-labeled group presents systematically higher values, with interquartile ranges that only marginally overlap, reflecting a proportionally larger eye area relative to the nose. \texttt{Nose\_vs\_Mouth} displays the opposite directional pattern — the male-labeled group has a higher median and a wider interquartile range, indicating a proportionally larger nose relative to the mouth. The inset corroborates the violin findings for \texttt{Nose\_vs\_Face} and \texttt{Mouth\_vs\_Face}, with the former remaining essentially uninformative and the latter showing only a slight central tendency difference. These two cross-region ratios, \texttt{Eyes\_vs\_Nose} and \texttt{Nose\_vs\_Mouth}, therefore emerge as the primary drivers of group proximity in this dataset.

The t-SNE projection of the full SI-FPM vectors provides a global view of the feature-space structure. The two reference groups are extensively interleaved across the two-dimensional embedding, with no clearly separable clusters. Some local tendencies toward grouping are visible, consistent with the directional ratio differences identified in the preceding plots, but they coexist with broad distributional overlap throughout the space. This picture corroborates the audit framing of SI-FPM: the metric surfaces population-level proportional tendencies while the pervasive overlap confirms that proximity to a reference group does not support individual-level classification.


\subsection{Generated-Image Audit Case Study}
\label{generated_case_study}

For each generated portrait, SI-FPM can compute its proximity to the two reference groups and display the top local drivers responsible for the decision. This format is particularly useful in AI ethics contexts because it does not merely output a category; it exposes the measurable facial ratios that contributed to the proximity score.

For each generated portrait, the audit panel combines the model output, the group-proximity scores, and the top three feature-level drivers defined in Section \ref{explainability}. \subsection{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{discussion}

SI-FPM offers a transparent alternative to purely embedding-based image comparison. Its main strength is that every feature has a direct geometric meaning, allowing researchers to inspect which proportions influence a similarity or group-proximity decision. This property is valuable for visual representation audits, where the legitimacy of the analysis depends not only on the output score but also on the ability to explain how that score was obtained.

At the same time, interpretability does not eliminate ethical risk. A transparent metric can still be misused if its outputs are framed as evidence of inherent social or biological categories. For this reason, SI-FPM should be restricted to controlled research and auditing contexts in which the reference groups, labels, datasets, and limitations are explicitly documented. In particular, results involving male/female labels should be interpreted as proximity to dataset annotations, not as inference of gender identity or biological sex.

Several technical limitations must also be considered. First, SI-FPM depends on segmentation quality. Incorrect or incomplete masks can distort area ratios, particularly for small regions such as eyes or eyebrows. In the group-level experiment, \texttt{Eyes\_vs\_Face} and \texttt{Eyes\_vs\_Nose} showed the widest distributional spread across both reference groups, which may partially reflect variability in eye segmentation quality in addition to genuine proportional differences. Images with low resolution, strong occlusion, or extreme pose are the most susceptible to this error mode, and their ratio vectors are stored with missing values rather than forced estimates. Second, imaging conditions — including expression, pose, lighting, and model-generated rendering artifacts — affect the segmented region boundaries even when the underlying facial proportions are stable. The cross-dataset heatmap revealed that some cross-individual similarity values were relatively high (e.g., Jackie Chan and Ayrton Senna at 0.46--0.53), which may reflect shared imaging conditions or expression states in the available image sets rather than genuinely similar facial proportions. Third, pairwise area ratios capture only one dimension of facial geometry. The group-level analysis showed that \texttt{Nose\_vs\_Face} carried little discriminative information in the IMDB-WIKI distributions, suggesting that area alone may be insufficient to characterize some proportional dimensions; distance-based or symmetry-based features may provide complementary evidence. Fourth, the current experiments are limited to binary dataset labels. The generated-image case study used the same two reference prototypes across all four models and both occupational prompts, which constrains the scope of the audit. Future work should evaluate reference structures that are broader, non-binary, and more carefully documented, including cases where labels are unavailable, uncertain, or not reducible to a two-group comparison.

Future ethnicity-related applications require additional care. Such analyses should be performed only when the research question is explicitly about aggregate representation auditing, the reference labels are ethically justified, and the results are framed as dataset- and context-specific. SI-FPM is not a method for inferring ethnicity from a face. Its appropriate role is to help researchers inspect whether visual systems produce measurable and explainable representation patterns under predefined audit conditions.


\section{Conclusion}
\label{conclusion}

This paper presented SI-FPM, a facial representation metric built from segmented region areas and pairwise proportional ratios. The method is interpretable by construction: each of its 21 features corresponds to a named anatomical region pair, every proximity score decomposes into named ratio contributions, and no post hoc surrogate model is required to produce an explanation. This design places SI-FPM within the tradition of inherently interpretable methods, extending it to the domain of facial representation auditing, where the ability to inspect and document the visual basis of a comparison is as important as the comparison result itself.

Three complementary experiments evaluated the method. In the identity-oriented analysis, SI-FPM prototype vectors maintained a consistent separation between the six public figures in the dataset, with off-diagonal similarity values ranging from 0.04 to 0.53 while within-individual similarity remained at its ceiling. In the group-level audit on IMDB-WIKI, \texttt{Eyes\_vs\_Nose} and \texttt{Nose\_vs\_Mouth} emerged as the primary proportional drivers of group proximity, while the t-SNE projection confirmed extensive distributional overlap between the two reference groups — a result that is consistent with the audit-oriented purpose of the method and incompatible with individual-level classification. In the generated-image case study, SI-FPM produced correct directional proximity assignments for all four generative models without any labeled training signal from the tested images, with proximity gaps ranging from 0.11 to 0.43 across models and occupational prompts, illustrating how the metric can surface proportional evidence of representational patterns in synthetic imagery.

Throughout the experiments, dataset-provided labels are treated as operational reference annotations, and proximity scores are interpreted as evidence of distributional patterns rather than claims about individual identity, gender, or biological category. This framing is not a caveat appended to the method: it is a constitutive design choice. SI-FPM is intended to make the assumptions and visual cues of an audit operationally visible so that researchers can evaluate, contest, and document them.

Several directions remain open. The current feature set is area-based; integrating distance-based and symmetry measurements could capture proportional dimensions that area alone does not resolve, as suggested by the low separability of \texttt{Nose\_vs\_Face} in the group-level experiment. Extending the reference structure beyond binary labels would broaden the scope of auditable comparisons to settings where categories are graded, multiple, or not predefined. Systematic evaluation of the driver-ranking procedure — including its stability across imaging conditions and its agreement with human-interpretable proportional judgments — is an important direction for future validation. Finally, applications of SI-FPM to ethnicity-related reference distributions require careful ethical justification and should be understood as aggregate auditing of representation patterns under externally defined labels, never as individual-level inference about ethnic or racial identity.
